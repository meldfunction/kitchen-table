§ 01 — Why Now

You're Paying More Than You Think

You know how your landlord owns the building but you're the one who makes it a home? That's your relationship with every tech subscription you pay for.

They own the servers. They own the data about you. They can raise prices, change terms, sell to a competitor, or comply with a government subpoena — and you have no say. You are a tenant in your own digital life. And the rent keeps going up.

The moment that changes is the moment enough people decide to own the table instead of renting a seat at it. That moment is now. The hardware is cheap enough. The software is good enough. The community model makes it possible for everyone.

"Every decade, the cost of compute drops enough that something previously only possible at institutional scale becomes possible at the kitchen table. The 2020s are giving us personal infrastructure."

§ 02 — The 101

New Words. Old Idea.

The concepts powering this are simple. You've seen them before — just not applied to your digital life. Click any question to expand.

Centralized AI (what you use now): You type a prompt. It travels over the internet to a server farm in Virginia or Texas. A corporation's computer processes it. The response comes back. Your prompt is logged, potentially used for training, and governed by their terms of service — which change whenever they want.

Decentralized AI (what we're building): You type a prompt. It goes to a computer in your home, or a neighbor's home, or your community's shared hardware. An open-weight AI model — weights that Meta, Microsoft, or Mistral have released publicly — processes it locally. Nothing leaves your network. No logging. No terms. No bill.

☁️ Their Cloud AI
Your prompts leave your device. Their servers process your requests. Your conversations may train their models. They decide what it can say. Prices go up. The model gets "aligned" to corporate interests. You can be cut off.
🏡 Your Local AI
Runs on hardware you own. Never phones home. Doesn't train anyone's model. Open weights you can inspect. No rate limits. No subscription. No "we updated our terms." Available when the internet is down.

The quality tradeoff is real but closing fast. Today's 8B open model handles 80% of what people use ChatGPT for. In 12 months, that number will be higher. The question isn't whether open models are good enough — it's whether you want to start building now or wait until the gap is even smaller.

A node is just a computer that's part of a network. In our context, it's usually a Raspberry Pi — a $80 credit-card-sized computer — sitting on a shelf in your home, running 24/7 on 5 watts of power (less than a phone charger).

One node can serve one person. Multiple nodes connected together serve the whole group and can collectively run things no single node could do alone. A group of 6 people each contributing a node creates 48GB of collective RAM — enough to run an AI model that no individual Pi could run by itself.

Think of it like a neighborhood water tower vs. everyone drilling their own well. The shared infrastructure is more capable, more resilient, and cheaper per person than anything each household could build alone.

A mesh network is a web of devices that can communicate directly with each other — without depending on a central server or internet connection. Instead of all traffic going through one hub (like your ISP's router), every device in a mesh can relay messages for every other device.

Why this matters: most "decentralized" systems still depend on the internet. If your ISP goes down, your VPN goes down, your cloud backup goes down. A true mesh doesn't care. It works over Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, or even radio signals between devices in range.

For community resilience: your local files, chat, and even AI inference can keep working when a storm takes out the internet, when you're camping, or when commercial infrastructure fails. The commercial internet becomes a nice-to-have, not a life-dependency.

A cooperative is an organization owned and governed by the people who use it. Your local credit union is a cooperative. REI is a cooperative. In a cooperative, members vote on decisions, share profits (or savings), and have a say in how things run.

Platform cooperativism applies this to technology: the platform is owned by its users, not venture capitalists. The governance is democratic. The benefits flow to members, not to a board room in San Francisco.

We use sociocracy as our governance model — a consent-based decision-making system where decisions are made by the people they affect, and nobody has unilateral authority. It's neither a dictatorship nor a chaotic democracy. It's structured consent.

Why does this matter for tech? Because who controls the infrastructure controls what you can do with it. Technology that's governed by its users will always make different choices than technology governed by shareholders seeking quarterly returns.

Local-first software stores your data on your device first, syncs to other devices second, and never requires a cloud service to function. You can work offline. Your data is yours by default, not the company's.

Obsidian (notes), Anytype (knowledge base), and a growing number of products are building this way because users are demanding it. They're proof that you can build beautiful, powerful, professional software that respects user ownership — and that people will pay for it without being surveilled.

We admire this approach deeply. Kitchen Table Cloud extends local-first from individual tools to community infrastructure — not just "your data on your device" but "your community's infrastructure on your community's hardware." The philosophy scales.

It depends on what role you want to play. Using the community services requires zero technical knowledge — you log in the same way you'd log into Google Drive or Spotify, just at a different address.

Setting up your own node requires comfort following step-by-step instructions and copying commands into a terminal window. If you can follow a recipe, you can do this. Our guides are written for exactly this level of comfort.

Being a Builder or technical contributor requires actual skill — but there's plenty of non-technical contribution too: writing documentation, helping onboard new members, translating guides, or simply being the person in your community who explains why this matters.

The most important thing is not technical skill — it's caring about who controls your digital life.

§ 06 — Community AI

What Happens When Your AI Belongs to All of You?

A solo node running a 3B model is useful. A community pod of six nodes running a distributed 13B model — trained on your collective knowledge — is something fundamentally different. This is where things get interesting.

Every major tech company runs AI on shared infrastructure. Google's AI doesn't run on one server — it runs across thousands, distributing the work, sharing the load, achieving capabilities no single machine could. There's no reason a community of friends and neighbors can't do the same thing.

The Petals protocol lets multiple nodes — each contributing a portion of a model's "layers" — collectively run an AI that's too large for any single device. A group of 6 people with Raspberry Pi 5s creates 48GB of collective RAM. That's enough to run Llama 3.1 8B at full precision: a model that handles tasks the small Pi models struggle with.

But the really interesting part isn't just raw capability. It's what becomes possible when the AI knows your community's context — fine-tuned on your collective knowledge, maintained by your pod, shaped by your values and not a corporate alignment team.

§ 08 — What Community Means To Us

We're Not Customers. We're Stewards.

Patagonia says: "We're in business to save our home planet." Everything they make either serves that mission or doesn't get made. The mission isn't marketing. It's load-bearing structure.

We're saying something similar about technology: we exist to demonstrate that digital infrastructure can be governed by the people who depend on it. Not as an ideological position. As a practical fact, proved one working service at a time.

The problem society isn't solving is this: the infrastructure that mediates modern life — communication, information, memory, intelligence — is privately owned and extractively operated. Governments regulate it poorly. Markets optimize it for profit. Users adapt to it. Nobody governs it for community benefit.

That's the gap we're standing in. Not to replace governments or markets — but to prove, at human scale, that communities can own and operate the infrastructure that serves them.

Infrastructure as Commons

Water was once privately controlled. So was the road network. Public pressure, over decades, made them into commons — governed for collective benefit. We believe digital infrastructure is the next commons waiting to happen. We're not waiting for governments to do it. We're doing it ourselves, first.

Participation, Not Consumption

Every tech company wants you to be a better customer. We want you to be a better participant. There's a meaningful difference between someone who uses a platform and someone who stewards one. Stewardship builds judgment, relationships, and resilience. Consumption extracts it.

Open Source as Civic Act

Open-source software is a form of commons-building — code that belongs to everyone, maintained by anyone, improved collectively. We see contributing to this ecosystem the same way previous generations saw voting, volunteering, or organizing. It's how you participate in the shape of your digital world.

The Problem Society Doesn't Solve

Governments regulate tech slowly and often in service of the companies they're supposed to regulate. Markets optimize for quarterly returns, not community wellbeing. Nonprofits orbit the edges without structural power to change the center.

What's missing is the cooperative layer. Organizations owned by their users, governed by consent, accountable to members rather than shareholders. This model — proven in banking (credit unions), retail (REI), agriculture, and housing — has never been systematically applied to digital infrastructure.

Kitchen Table Cloud is a bet that it should be. We're small enough to move fast, principled enough to stay honest, and practical enough to know that the best argument for cooperative infrastructure is cooperative infrastructure that actually works.

We want to change minds by changing what's possible. One working service at a time. One community at a time. Starting here.

§ 12 — For the Technically Curious

The Stack

If these words mean something to you, welcome home. If not — skip to the repo, where we have guides written for humans.

🤖 AI Inference Layer

Ollama serves quantized open-weight models via OpenAI-compatible API. Petals enables distributed inference across mesh nodes. llama.cpp for benchmarking and fine-tuning prep. Unsloth for Pi-friendly fine-tuning on group data.

  • → ollama.com — primary model runtime
  • → petals.dev — distributed inference protocol
  • → llama.cpp — CPU-optimized inference engine

🌐 Mesh & Config

Tailscale (WireGuard) encrypted overlay across all nodes regardless of ISP or NAT. Yggdrasil for fully trustless mesh with zero central authority. Ansible pushes config to all nodes in one command.

  • → tailscale.com — encrypted mesh network
  • → yggdrasil — fully decentralized mesh
  • → ansible — config push to all nodes

📦 Self-Hosted Services

CasaOS Docker app store. NextCloud AIO. Navidrome via Subsonic API. Jellyfin for video. Matrix/Synapse federated messaging. Home Assistant for automation. SearXNG meta-search. Vaultwarden password vault. Pi-hole + Unbound DNS.

  • → casaos.io — home server dashboard
  • → nextcloud.com — files, calendar, photos
  • → home-assistant.io — local smart home

📡 Off-Grid Comms

Meshtastic LoRa radios (~$35) for text mesh with no internet or towers. ~5km range, longer on open terrain. Reticulum network stack for cryptographic mesh over any medium. OsmAnd for offline maps.

  • → meshtastic.org — off-grid radio mesh
  • → reticulum.network — cryptographic mesh protocol
  • → osmand.net — offline maps

§ 13 — About Us

We Built This Because We Need It

Kitchen Table Cloud is not a startup. It's not funded by venture capital. It doesn't have a growth target or an exit strategy. It exists because a small group of people looked at the digital infrastructure they depended on and didn't like what they saw.

We come from different backgrounds — organizing, technology, design, education, community cooperative development. What we share is a conviction that the extractive model of digital services is not inevitable, and that building the alternative is more useful than criticizing the status quo.

We're inspired by Obsidian's commitment to local-first data, Anytype's privacy-as-architecture philosophy, Elicit's honesty about what AI can and cannot do, and the Calm Tech Institute's insistence that technology should serve human attention rather than harvest it.

We're trying to build at the intersection of platform cooperativism, sociocracy, and practical open-source technology. The governance model is consent-based. The economics are cooperative. The technology is open. The community is the product — and the product belongs to the community.

If this resonates with you, the best thing you can do is show up. Open a discussion. Ask a question. Tell someone this exists. Or just run the three commands above and cancel a subscription. Every lever you take back matters.

What We Believe

Technology that connects without entrapping is possible. The extractive model is a design choice, not a law of nature.

Cooperative governance produces better infrastructure than corporate governance — for the people who actually use it.

The kitchen table is the right metaphor. Not the server room. Not the boardroom. The place where communities make decisions together.

The time is now. Open models are capable. Hardware is cheap. The window between "impossible for individuals" and "captured by corporations" is open. It won't stay open forever.

Open Source

Everything we build is open. Everything we use is open. Transparency isn't a feature — it's the model.

Cooperative

Governed by members, for members. Decisions by consent. Benefits stay with the community.

Local-First

Data lives close to the people it belongs to. Cloud is optional. Resilience is structural.

Human Scale

We build for communities of 5–500 people. Not millions. Depth over breadth.
